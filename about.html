Architect and PhD candidate, my work focuses on supporting residents (especially of working-class and marginalized neighborhoods) in reclaiming agency over the transformation of their environments, through collaborative and sustainable design practices.
My research analyzes the limits of traditional participatory processes in architecture that confine users to participation forms aligned with neoliberal logics. Instead, I explore situated and continuous forms of design that allow spaces to evolve over time, embracing use, unpredictability, and collective agency as active forces of transformation.
In this approach, the architect is not a top-down planner but a facilitator of local dynamics, an enabler and grassroots architect who works alongside residents to support the reclaiming and reimagining of their environments. Inhabitant-driven forms of resistance are understood as acts of defiance against dominant architectural production logics.
My research also questions the use of digital tools in architecture, which are often centered on formal planning and top-down processes. I advocate for an open-ended approach that embraces indeterminacy, reimagining the role of digital tools to support more inclusive and situated design practices.
This research ultimately challenges extractive models of urban renewal based on demolition and reconstruction. It asks how long-term, adaptive transformations rooted in everyday use, care, and collective imagination can redefine both the practice of architecture, and the political role of the architect.
